## Q.11) Examine the role of the supreme court as the final interpreter of the Indian constitution. (2013) (15)

### üéØ Core Theme
The Supreme Court's role as final constitutional interpreter‚Äîanchored in Article 32 ("heart and soul")‚Äîencompasses judicial review for basic structure preservation, federal dispute resolution, progressive fundamental rights expansion (Maneka Gandhi, Navtej Singh Johar), and active living Constitution shaping, balancing textual fidelity with societal evolution.

### üîë Key Recall Terms

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Supreme Court holds pivotal role as final constitutional interpreter
- Responsibility at heart of democratic framework and judicial system
- Enshrined primarily under Article 32
- Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: "heart and soul of Constitution"
- Empowers direct approach for fundamental rights enforcement
- Entrusts Court with guardian role of rights
- Function encompasses several key aspects
- Ensures Constitution supremacy, rule of law, rights protection

**Body:**

*Judicial Review Doctrine:*
- Central to Court's role as final interpreter
- Not explicitly mentioned but derived from Constitution provisions
- Allows Court to invalidate laws violating Constitution
- Executive actions also subject to review
- Keshavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) landmark
- Asserted authority to review and strike down amendments
- Threatening basic structure alteration
- Preserving foundational principles: separation of powers, rule of law, fundamental rights

*Federal Dispute Resolution:*
- Interpretative jurisdiction extends to Centre-State power allocation
- Ensuring federal balance
- Smooth functioning of quasi-federal structure
- Arbitrates conflicts between government levels
- Safeguarding federal principles enshrined in Constitution

*Progressive Rights Expansion:*
- Expanding and evolving fundamental rights understanding
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)
- Expanded right to life under Article 21
- Include right to live with dignity
- Broadening rights to meet contemporary needs
- Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018)
- Homosexuality decriminalization
- Supreme Court interpretation leading societal changes
- Dynamic constitutional interpretation adapting to evolving values

*Proactive Role:*
- Not just negating unconstitutional laws
- Proactively defining contours of rights, duties, principles
- Delicate balance between upholding text and spirit
- Responding to society's changing needs

**Conclusion:**
Supreme Court through interpretations and judgments actively shapes living Constitution; role as final interpreter fundamental to ensuring Constitution remains living document, responsive to times' aspirations and challenges; responsibility underscores Court's central position in democracy, safeguarding rule of law, protecting citizens' rights, mediating power dynamics within state machinery.

## Q.12) Examine the debate on the appointment procedure of judges to the higher judiciary in India. (2014) (15)

### üéØ Core Theme
The judicial appointment debate centers on balancing independence with accountability‚ÄîCollegium system (established through Three Judges Cases) criticized for opacity and favoritism yet defended for protecting judicial independence; NJAC proposal struck down (2015) as unconstitutional; ongoing search for middle ground ensuring transparency without executive interference.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Judicial Appointments | Collegium System | Three Judges Cases | 1982, 1993, 1998 | Chief Justice of India | Upendra Baxi | J.S. Khehar | NJAC | National Judicial Appointments Commission | 99th Amendment | Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association 2015 | Arghya Sengupta | Transparency | Accountability | Judicial Independence

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Focal point of contention among scholars, politicians, civil society
- Crux: Balancing independence with accountability
- Especially Supreme Court and High Courts
- Method of appointment central debate

**Body:**

*Collegium System Origins:*
- Current procedure governed by Collegium system
- Evolved through Three Judges Cases (1982, 1993, 1998)
- Consists of CJI and four senior-most SC judges
- Make recommendations for appointments and transfers
- Established to maintain judiciary independence
- Minimizing executive role in appointment process

*Criticism of Collegium:*
- Upendra Baxi: Lacks transparency and accountability
- Susceptible to biases and favoritism
- Absence of formalized process and criteria
- Led to opacity and exclusivity criticisms
- Calls for reforms introducing transparency
- Objective criteria without compromising independence

*Defense of Collegium:*
- Former CJI J.S. Khehar: Necessary for independence protection
- From executive interference
- Executive involvement could undermine judiciary's check on executive power

*NJAC Proposal and Rejection:*
- Proposed to replace Collegium system
- 99th Constitutional Amendment Act
- National Judicial Appointments Commission Act 2014
- Body comprising: CJI, two senior SC judges, Union Law Minister, two eminent persons
- Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs. Union of India (2015)
- Struck down NJAC as unconstitutional
- Ruled it impinged on judiciary's independence

*Post-NJAC Debate:*
- Court's decision fueled further debate
- Some: Reaffirmation of judicial independence
- Others: Missed opportunity for balanced, transparent system
- Arghya Sengupta: Need for middle ground
- Ensuring both independence and accountability
- Improved NJAC version addressing SC concerns

**Conclusion:**
Debate reflects broader concerns about ensuring judicial independence while enhancing transparency and accountability; underscores ongoing struggle finding optimal balance between competing priorities strengthening judiciary's role in democratic society.

## Q.13) Critically analyse the discretionary powers granted to the governor by the Indian constitution. (2015) (15)

### üéØ Core Theme
Governor's discretionary powers under Article 163‚Äîappointing CM without clear majority, recommending President's Rule (Article 356)‚Äîface critique for potential misuse favoring central government over constitutional propriety; S.R. Bommai judgment (1994) imposed guidelines; tension between constitutional safeguard role and federalism preservation requires judicial oversight and clearer directives.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Governor | Discretionary Powers | Article 163 | Article 356 | President's Rule | Chief Minister Appointment | Granville Austin | S.R. Bommai 1994 | Floor Test | Subhash C. Kashyap | Center-State Relations | Constitutional Crisis | Federalism | Judicial Guidelines

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Subject of intense debate and scrutiny
- Implications for center-state relations
- Functioning of parliamentary democracy at state level
- Constitution envisages Governor as nominal head
- Acting on aid and advice of Council of Ministers
- Also vests certain discretionary powers
- Mechanism to uphold constitutional governance

**Body:**

*Constitutional Basis:*
- Article 163: Governor can exercise discretionary powers
- Situations not defined by law or Constitution
- Interpreted to include: CM appointment without clear majority
- Recommending President's Rule (Article 356)
- If state cannot run per Constitution
- Seeking information from CM on administrative/legislative matters

*Critique of Misuse:*
- Granville Austin: Potential for power abuse
- Distorting balance between center and states
- Often acting on behalf of central government
- Rather than neutral constitutional functionaries
- CM appointment particularly contentious
- Instances favoring central government preferences
- Over spirit of democracy and federalism

*President's Rule Controversy:*
- Recommendation for President's Rule significant contention
- S.R. Bommai vs. Union of India (1994) landmark
- Response to arbitrary state government dismissal
- Supreme Court laid down strict guidelines
- Prevent misuse of Article 356
- Power should be exercised judiciously, not capriciously
- Emphasizing floor test supremacy in determining majority

*Defense of Discretionary Powers:*
- Proponents: Necessary for constitutional safeguard
- Ensure governance per Constitution
- In political instability or constitutional crises
- Governor's role crucial preserving governance integrity

*Scholarly Analysis:*
- Subhash C. Kashyap: Powers necessary but need caution
- Exercise with transparency, adhering to constitutional principles
- Maintain democratic fabric
- Debate reflects broader federalism concerns
- Need for clearer constitutional guidelines
- Prevent misuse undermining state autonomy

**Conclusion:**
While discretionary powers designed ensuring constitutional propriety, application subject to controversy; need careful balance between Governor's constitutional role and federalism/democracy principles; critical discourse underscores judicial oversight importance, clear directives, commitment to democratic norms in power exercise.

### üìù Answer Structure

- Sparked significant debate among scholars, policymakers, public

**Body:**

*Reduced Session Productivity:*
- Frequency and productivity of sessions concerning

*Diminished Debate Quality:*
- Rise of partisanship, decline in cross-party dialogue

- Voice votes without detailed committee scrutiny
- Suhas Palshikar: Bypassing parliamentary committees
- Detrimental to legislation quality and democratic accountability

*Legislative Absenteeism:*
- Declining participation of members in proceedings
- Questions about elected representatives' commitment
- Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR) reports
- Highlight trend, underscore need for greater accountability
- MP participation concerns

*Weakened Accountability Mechanisms:*
- Question Hour and Zero Hour weakening
- Critical mechanisms for holding government accountable
- Restrictions limit opportunities to raise concerns

- Particularly Prime Minister's Office
- Reducing Parliament's role in governance and decision-making

*Resilience and Adaptability:*
- Parliament also shown resilience
- Efforts to enhance functioning
- Live telecasts by Lok Sabha TV and Rajya Sabha TV
- Increase transparency and public engagement
- Instances where debate/scrutiny led to amendments
- Capacity to function as democratic deliberation site

**Conclusion:**
While genuine concerns regarding decline, remains crucial institution; addressing challenges requires strengthening procedures, enhancing debate quality, ensuring greater accountability; revitalization as effective, vibrant democratic institution imperative for democracy's health.

## Q.15) Do the lieutenant governors have more power than the governor of the states? Explain. (2018) (15)

### üéØ Core Theme
Lieutenant Governors in UTs with legislatures (especially Delhi under Article 239AA) have complex, pronounced governance roles compared to state Governors‚ÄîSC judgment (Government of NCT vs. Union of India 2018) clarified LG must act on CoM advice except land, police, public order; LG's referral power to President creates unique balance; context-specific "more power" notion reflects UTs' special constitutional status and Union's direct interest.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Lieutenant Governor | LG | Union Territories | UTs | Article 239AA | 69th Amendment 1991 | Delhi | Governor | Articles 153-167 | Government of NCT of Delhi vs. Union of India 2018 | Land, Police, Public Order | Referral to President | Federal Structure | Constitutional Framework

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Interesting study in Indian federalism nuances
- Administrative governance complexities
- Constitution and judicial pronouncements delineate powers
- Similarities and distinctions
- Influenced by specific frameworks governing states and UTs

**Body:**

*Governor's Role in States:*
- Outlined in Articles 153 to 167
- Governors act as nominal state head
- Real executive power with CM and Council of Ministers
- Powers include: CM appointment, bill assent
- Under circumstances, recommend President's Rule

*LG's Powers in UTs with Legislatures:*
- Defined under Article 239AA
- Introduced by 69th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1991 (for Delhi)
- Government of NCT of Delhi vs. Union of India (2018) landmark judgment
- Clarified constitutional position and powers of Delhi LG
- LG acts on aid and advice of Delhi government CoM
- In all matters except land, police, public order
- Unique role: Referring matters with difference of opinion to President

*Comparative Analysis:*
- LGs in UTs with legislatures have more complex role
- Than Governors in states
- Given Union Territories' special constitutional status
- Governors: Bridge between state and central governments
- LGs in UTs like Delhi: More pronounced governance role
- Union's direct interest, coordination on national importance matters

*Context-Specific Power:*
- Notion that LGs possess "more power" context-specific
- Primarily applying to administrative and legislative matters
- In UTs with assemblies
- Particularly evident in Delhi's administrative structure
- LG's concurrence pivotal in several executive functions
- Creates unique balance of power
- Necessitates careful negotiation between UT government and LG office

**Conclusion:**
While LG powers may appear more pronounced in certain aspects compared to state Governors, essential to understand within broader constitutional framework; designed to address unique administrative needs of UTs; Supreme Court interpretations and rulings elucidate balance, ensuring UT governance aligns with federalism and democracy principles enshrined in Constitution.

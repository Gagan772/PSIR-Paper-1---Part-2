## Q.11) Comment on Increasingly higher focus on Directive Principles of State Policy. (2014)

### üéØ Core Theme

### üîë Key Recall Terms

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- DPSP enshrined in Part IV as guidelines for state
- Aimed at guiding state policy towards social and economic welfare
- While not justiciable (cannot be enforced by courts), fundamental in governance
- Acting as guidelines for securing economic and social democracy
- Encompass wide range of goals: social/economic welfare, education, environmental protection, peace and security

**Body:**

*Role in Development Vision:*
- Growing focus attributed to shaping comprehensive vision for India's development
- Bridge gap between Fundamental Rights (Part III) and achieving just, equitable society
- Granville Austin: DPSP and Fundamental Rights as "conscience of the Constitution"
- Highlighting importance in realizing Constitution makers' aspirations

*Yardstick for Government Policies:*
- Increasingly become measure for assessing government policies
- Commitment to welfare-oriented governance
- Policies on environmental sustainability, workers' rights, education reforms guided by DPSP
- Emphasis on rural development, right to work, education for all, public health initiatives
- Direct reflections of DPSP in governmental agendas

*Judicial Role in Elevating DPSP:*
- Judiciary played significant role through interpretative activism
- While DPSP not enforceable, judiciary relied on them to guide decisions
- Cases related to environmental jurisprudence, social welfare, economic justice
- Unnikrishnan JP vs. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993)
- Supreme Court emphasized right to education concomitant to Fundamental Rights
- Illustrates how DPSP influence interpretation of justiciable rights

*Scholarly Perspective:*
- Amartya Sen and Jean Dr√®ze underscored importance for achieving substantive democracy
- Development should be measured not just by economic growth
- But by enhancement of living standards, social opportunities, freedom individuals enjoy
- DPSP with focus on social welfare and economic equality align with broader understanding of development

*Shift in Governance Priorities:*
- Increasing focus reflects shift towards governance prioritizing socio-economic equity
- Sustainability and well-being of citizens
- Understanding that true development not merely about economic growth
- Building society that is equitable, just, and inclusive
- Indicative of Indian state's commitment to uphold Fundamental Rights AND strive towards DPSP ideals
- Working towards holistic development of nation and people

**Conclusion:**
Increasing focus on DPSP reflects shift towards governance prioritizing socio-economic equity, sustainability, and citizen well-being, signifying understanding that true development is about building equitable, just, and inclusive society, working towards holistic development of nation and people.

## Q.12) Analyse the significance of Article 32 of the Indian Constitution. (2013)

### üéØ Core Theme
Article 32, termed "heart and soul" of Constitution by Ambedkar, provides right to constitutional remedies empowering citizens to directly approach Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights, embodying principles of justice, liberty, and rule of law.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Article 32 | Heart and Soul | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar | Right to Constitutional Remedies | Writs | Habeas Corpus | Mandamus | Prohibition | Quo Warranto | Certiorari | Granville Austin | Kesavananda Bharati | Maneka Gandhi | Judicial Review | Upendra Baxi

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Article 32 referred to as "heart and soul" of Constitution by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar
- Testament to importance of judicial protection of individual rights in democratic polity
- Provides right to constitutional remedies
- Enables individuals to move Supreme Court directly for enforcement of fundamental rights
- Significance lies in safeguarding citizens' liberties AND maintaining democratic ethos and constitutional governance

**Body:**

*Foundational Premise - Writ Jurisdiction:*
- Empowerment of Supreme Court to issue writs:
  - Habeas Corpus (personal liberty)
  - Mandamus (compel public duty)
  - Prohibition (prevent excess jurisdiction)
  - Quo Warranto (check unauthorized office holding)
  - Certiorari (quash illegal orders)
- Enforcement of fundamental rights
- Direct appellate route to Supreme Court
- Underscores Constitution framers' intent to place protection of individual rights at forefront
- Ensures expedited and efficacious mechanism for redressal of grievances

*Granville Austin's Analysis:*
- In "The Indian Constitution: Cornerstone of a Nation"
- Underscores centrality of Article 32 in constitutional framework
- Exemplifies Constitution's commitment to individual rights
- Analysis illuminates procedural and substantive dimensions
- Emphasizes role in facilitating access to justice and acting as check on state power

*Landmark Judicial Reinforcement:*
- Supreme Court reinforced significance through jurisprudence
- Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973):
  - Underscored importance of fundamental rights
  - Declared basic structure of Constitution, including Article 32, cannot be amended
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978):
  - Expanded interpretation of Article 21
  - Enhanced scope of rights protected under Article 32

*Principle of Judicial Review:*
- Article 32 embodies principle of judicial review
- Crucial mechanism for overseeing constitutionality of legislative and executive actions
- Essential for preserving balance of power among branches of government
- Upholding rule of law

*Challenges to Efficacy:*
- Limitations in access to justice for marginalized communities
- Instances of judicial reluctance in face of executive actions
- Upendra Baxi critiqued these limitations
- Called for more proactive role by judiciary in safeguarding rights
- Ensuring justice accessible to all sections of society

**Conclusion:**
Article 32 holds paramount importance in India's constitutional democracy, empowering citizens to protect fundamental rights while embodying principles of justice, liberty, and rule of law, ensuring Constitution remains living document responsive to citizens' needs and rights.

## Q.13) The Constitution of India is the 'cornerstone of a nation' (Granville Austin). Analyze. (2023)

### üéØ Core Theme
Granville Austin's characterization captures the Constitution's profound foundational role as both legal skeleton structuring government and moral compass guiding democratic spirit, enabling India to balance unity with diversity, rights with welfare, and change with continuity.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Cornerstone of Nation | Granville Austin | Federal Structure | Articles 1-11 | Seventh Schedule | Preamble | Fundamental Rights (Arts 12-35) | Directive Principles (Arts 36-51) | 42nd Amendment | Mini-Constitution | Kesavananda Bharati | Basic Structure | Articles 32 & 226 | Judicial Review | Articles 29-30 | Pluralistic Society

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Granville Austin's description as "cornerstone of a nation" encapsulates profound significance
- Foundational role in governance, identity, and ethos of India
- Drafted after independence from British rule
- Serves as legal skeleton structuring government
- Also moral compass guiding democratic spirit and social justice goals

**Body:**

*Intricate Governance Structure:*
- Adopted January 26, 1950
- Reflects diverse aspirations of people
- Balances strong centralized authority with regional autonomy
- Federal structure in Articles 1-11 and Seventh Schedule
- Divides legislative powers between Union and States
- Crucial for nation of India's vast geographical and cultural diversity
- Enables both unity and local self-governance

*Preamble as Guiding Ideals:*
- Sets forth ideals of justice, liberty, equality, fraternity
- Not just lofty aspirations but actionable mandates
- Guided India's legal and social evolution
- Fundamental Rights (Articles 12-35): commitment to individual liberties
- Directive Principles (Articles 36-51): ethical and governance standards for social welfare

*Dynamic Instrument - Amendments:*
- Austin emphasizes role in mediating social change and fostering national unity
- Amenable to amendments reflecting changing socio-political landscape
- 42nd Amendment: "Mini-Constitution"
- Expanded Directive Principles
- Made Constitution more explicit in commitment to socialist and secular state
- Reflected government's agenda at the time

*Judicial Role - Interpretation & Protection:*
- Judiciary through power of judicial review (Articles 32 & 226)
- Pivotal role in interpreting Constitution
- Ensures democratic ethos and fundamental rights preserved
- Kesavananda Bharati case (1973):
  - Established doctrine of basic structure
  - Certain fundamental features cannot be altered/destroyed by amendments
  - Safeguarding democratic values and rule of law

*Accommodation of Pluralism:*
- Articles 29 and 30 protect rights of minorities
- Ensures India's diversity is source of strength rather than division
- Enabled various cultural, linguistic, religious communities to thrive
- While fostering sense of Indian identity

**Conclusion:**
Constitution's central role in shaping India's polity, society, and governance; provided framework for democratic governance, safeguarded individual rights, facilitated social transformation and national integration; testament to visionary leaders; continues as living document evolving with nation.

## Q.14) The main goal of the Fundamental Duties in the Indian Constitution is to generate civic responsibility among the citizens. Explain. (2023)

### üéØ Core Theme
Fundamental Duties, added via 42nd Amendment (1976) in Part IV-A, serve as critical mechanism for fostering civic responsibility, complementing rights with duties, and balancing individual freedoms with collective responsibilities essential for vibrant democracy.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Fundamental Duties | Part IV-A | 42nd Amendment 1976 | Granville Austin | Conscience | Article 51-A | Civic Consciousness | Social Responsibility | Sovereignty and Integrity | Social Harmony | Political Participation | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar | Scientific Temper | Jawaharlal Nehru | Non-enforceable | Moral Framework

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Fundamental Duties serve as critical mechanism for fostering civic responsibility
- Granville Austin: duties act as "conscience" for citizens and nation
- Reinforce commitment towards collective welfare and democratic fabric
- Embedded in Part IV-A (added by 42nd Amendment Act 1976)
- Complement Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles

**Body:**

*Complementary Relationship:*
- Emphasize reciprocal relationship between rights and duties
- Balance between individual rights and collective responsibilities
- Fundamental to functioning of vibrant democracy like India's

*Core Goal - Civic Consciousness:*
- Instill sense of civic consciousness and social responsibility
- Reflected in specific duties:
  - Article 51-A(a): Upholding sovereignty and integrity of India
  - Article 51-A(e): Promoting social harmony
  - Article 51-A(e): Renouncing practices derogatory to dignity of women
- Underscore importance of citizens actively contributing to preservation of just and harmonious society

*Political Participation:*
- Promote engagement in democratic processes
- Article 51-A(g): Encourages voting in elections and fulfilling role as active participants
- Dr. B.R. Ambedkar emphasized: "Political democracy cannot last unless there lies at base of it social democracy"
- Fundamental Duties reinforce this by urging active participation in democratic machinery

*Scientific Temper and Reform:*
- Article 51-A(h): Develop scientific temper, humanism, spirit of inquiry and reform
- Instrumental in shaping informed and rational citizenry
- Jawaharlal Nehru believed scientific temper vital for India's progress
- Duty reflects that vision

*Nature and Impact:*
- While not enforceable by law, provide moral and ethical framework
- Complement legal rights and freedoms enjoyed by citizens
- Indian judiciary observed: "not only pillars for orderly and disciplined growth but also means for progress"
- Serve as reminder of responsibilities towards nation and its values

**Conclusion:**
Main goal is generating civic responsibility among citizens; play pivotal role in shaping character, fostering social cohesion, strengthening democratic ethos; while not legally enforceable, serve as moral compass guiding citizens as active participants in nation's progress and custodians of values.

## Q.15) Discuss the efficacy of judicial review in India. (2015)

### üéØ Core Theme
Judicial review, vested by Articles 13, 32, 136, 226, and 227, has proven effective in preserving constitutional supremacy, protecting fundamental rights, and maintaining balance of power, though challenged by case backlogs and debates over judicial activism versus restraint.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Judicial Review | Articles 13, 32, 136, 226, 227 | Constitutional Supremacy | Kesavananda Bharati | Basic Structure Doctrine | Indira Gandhi vs. Raj Narain | Maneka Gandhi | Rajeev Dhavan | PIL (Public Interest Litigation) | Pratap Bhanu Mehta | T.N. Godavarman | Environmental Governance | Judicial Activism | Judicial Overreach

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Judicial review stands as cornerstone of Constitution
- Empowers judiciary to examine constitutionality of legislative and executive actions
- Vested by Articles 13, 32, 136, 226, and 227
- Ensures all laws and actions adhere to constitutional framework
- Efficacy demonstrated through preserving Constitution's supremacy, protecting fundamental rights, maintaining balance of power

**Body:**

*Landmark Case - Kesavananda Bharati (1973):*
- Most celebrated example of judicial review
- Supreme Court established doctrine of basic structure
- Parliament has wide powers to amend Constitution
- BUT cannot alter its basic structure
- Underscored judiciary's role in safeguarding Constitution's fundamental tenets
- Against potential legislative and executive overreach

*Indira Gandhi vs. Raj Narain (1975):*
- Significant instance of judicial review
- Led to invalidation of election of then-Prime Minister Indira Gandhi
- Illustrated judiciary's power to scrutinize even highest levels of government

*Maneka Gandhi vs. Union of India (1978):*
- Expanded scope of personal liberty and due process under Article 21
- Showcased judiciary's capacity to reinterpret and expand fundamental rights
- In light of changing societal values

*Scholarly Perspectives - Positive View:*
- Rajeev Dhavan (noted legal scholar) praised Indian judiciary's activism
- Particularly in Public Interest Litigation (PIL)
- Extension of judicial review enabling courts to address concerns of marginalized sections directly

*Critical Perspective:*
- Pratap Bhanu Mehta argued judicial overreach can lead to encroachments
- On domains of legislature and executive
- Raises concerns about balance of powers

*Environmental Governance:*
- Judiciary played crucial role through judicial review
- T.N. Godavarman Thirumulpad vs. Union of India
- Expanded definition of forests and imposed restrictions on use
- Reflects judiciary's proactive stance in environmental protection

*Challenges to Efficacy:*
- Backlog of cases and delays in judiciary
- Can hinder timely resolution of constitutional matters
- Impacts effectiveness of judicial review
- Fine balance between judicial activism and restraint remains contentious
- Debates on judiciary's role in policy-making processes

**Conclusion:**
Judicial review has been effective tool in upholding Constitution's supremacy, protecting fundamental rights, ensuring separation of powers; while facing challenges, its role in fostering accountability and constitutional governance has been invaluable; continuing evolution through landmark judgments reflects judiciary's adaptability and commitment to constitutional values.

### üéØ Core Theme

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Judicial principle: Constitution has certain basic features
- Cannot be altered/destroyed through parliamentary amendments
- First articulated in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

- Aimed to ensure essential features remain inviolable

**Body:**

*Kesavananda Bharati Ruling:*
- Supreme Court ruled: Parliament has wide powers under Article 368
- BUT cannot alter basic structure or framework
- Pivotal judgment placing limitation on Parliament's amendatory powers
- Previously thought to be unlimited
- Ensures fundamental identity remains preserved against potential misuse

*Features of Basic Structure:*
- Not explicitly defined in Constitution itself
- Supreme Court clarified through various rulings over time
- Identified features: Supremacy of Constitution, Rule of law, Separation of powers
- Preamble objectives (justice, liberty, equality, fraternity)
- Judicial review
- Federalism

*Scholarly Reception:*
- Granville Austin: "Most important constitutional case in Indian history"
- "Providing pledge for India's democratic government"
- Critics like Upendra Baxi: Doctrine grants excessive power to judiciary
- Over democratically elected legislature
- Potentially leading to judicial overreach

*Subsequent Applications:*
- Minerva Mills case (1980)
- Court struck down clauses of 42nd Amendment Act (1976)
- Seen as violative of basic structure
- Tilted balance of power too heavily toward Parliament over judiciary
- Doctrine invoked to reaffirm judicial review power
- Maintain balance of power between government branches

**Conclusion:**
Serves as safeguard for democratic values and foundational aspects; ensures short-term political goals or majoritarian legislative actions don't compromise essential philosophical and structural elements of nation's fundamental law.
